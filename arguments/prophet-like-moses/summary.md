# Prophet Like Moses

## Central Thesis
[anchored] Deuteronomy 18:15-22 is the repository's primary Torah-side prophecy cluster. [synthesis] It creates pressure by joining office, speech, and accountability: God raises the prophet, the prophet is like Moses, the prophet speaks received divine words, and the hearer is answerable for refusing that speech.

## Why These Passages Belong Together
[Deuteronomy 18:15-22](../../bible/ot/deuteronomy/ch18/v15-22/summary.md) supplies the promise and the prophetic-test frame. [Deuteronomy 34:10](../../bible/ot/deuteronomy/ch34/v10/summary.md) keeps the Moses-like category open or contested inside the Torah horizon. [John 1:19-21](../../bible/nt/john/ch1/v19-21/summary.md) shows that “the Prophet” could be treated as a recognizable expectation category. [Acts 3:21](../../bible/nt/acts/ch3/v21/summary.md) keeps Peter's use of Deuteronomy 18 inside a future-restoration frame, while [Acts 3:22-23](../../bible/nt/acts/ch3/v22-23/summary.md) preserves the major Christian application to Jesus.

## Cumulative Profile
The cluster does not merely say “a prophet will come.” Its force is that a candidate must satisfy several constraints at once: a Moses-like public office, words received from God, covenantal accountability for response, and a plausible reading of “from their brothers.” The Horeb background matters because the promise answers Israel's request for mediated revelation rather than immediate exposure to the divine voice. The Ishmaelite support pages do not prove the claim, but they matter because they test whether that final constraint can extend beyond an Israel-only reading.

## Convergence Test
- Deuteronomy 18:15-19 gives the positive promise: raised prophet, likeness to Moses, divine words in the mouth, and required obedience.
- Deuteronomy 18:16-17 grounds that promise in Israel's Horeb request for mediated revelation and God's approval of that request.
- Deuteronomy 18:20-22 gives the negative control: presumption and failed speech test prophecy claims rather than identifying the candidate.
- Deuteronomy 34:10 prevents a quick claim that the Moses-like category was already exhausted.
- John 1:19-21 keeps “the Prophet” distinct from Messiah and Elijah in an expectation scene.
- Acts 3:21-23 confirms Deuteronomy 18 remained live, while also forcing engagement with the Jesus-centered reading inside a speech that still looks toward future restoration.

## Strongest Alternative Synthesis
The strongest rival synthesis says Deuteronomy 18 addresses Israel's need for prophets instead of divination, that the prophet must arise from within Israel's covenant community, that Deuteronomy 34:10 closes Moses' singularity rather than reopening it, and that Acts 3 authoritatively identifies the passage with Jesus. This rival reading is not a footnote; it is a control on the whole cluster.

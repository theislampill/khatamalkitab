# John 14:30-31

`Status:` anchored. `Role:` context and control; not direct identification by itself. `Contribution:` control; keeps the later-guide material inside Johannine mission and dependence.

## Claim

This passage contributes ruler-of-the-world and obedience-departure context around Jesus' mission. It matters as Paraclete context and subordination control because it frames the farewell material in terms of conflict, mission, and Jesus' obedience to the Father rather than in terms of abstract metaphysics.

## Reasoning

Taken as a range, the passage clarifies what kind of world the later-guide promise inhabits. Jesus is leaving, the ruler of the world is active, and Jesus' own mission is presented through obedience to the Father's command. That matters because it keeps dependence, sending, and conflict in the foreground. Any attempt to interpret the later-guide material has to account for that setting. The passage therefore functions as a control on both over-spiritual readings and careless later-guide claims.

The mistake this page prevents is abstraction. It is tempting to pull the Paraclete promises away from their farewell setting and treat them as a free-floating doctrinal label. John 14:30-31 resists that move. It keeps the promises inside a scene of departure, obedience, conflict, and love for the Father. That setting matters because it shows the kind of mission logic the wider Gospel-side case has to inherit.

## Local Significance

Read as a passage, this range has more force than its atom pages read separately because the two verses hold together departure, opposition, and obedience. The local point is not simply that Jesus is leaving. It is that his departure belongs to a mission defined by love for the Father and by doing what he has been commanded. That is exactly the sort of dependence language the wider Gospel-side case must keep in view.

## Networked Significance

This page chiefly strengthens [Paraclete And Subordination](../../../../../arguments/paraclete-and-subordination/summary.md). It also stabilizes [Paraclete](../../../../../arguments/paraclete/summary.md) by showing that the later-guide material is embedded in a discourse of obedience, departure, and conflict. The range does not identify the thesis by itself, but it keeps the Gospel-side argument from losing contact with John's own scene logic.

That makes the page a real control rather than a decorative caution. By keeping mission and dependence visible at this exact point in the discourse, it helps the later-guide line sound more textually native and less like an external theory laid over John.

John 14:30-31 strengthens the thesis indirectly by keeping the Paraclete argument anchored in Johannine mission and dependence categories. That control makes the larger Gospel-side case more credible.

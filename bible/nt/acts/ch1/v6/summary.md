# Acts 1:6

`Status:` anchored. `Role:` restoration-expectation anchor after the resurrection. `Contribution:` support for live expectation and anti-closure.

## Claim
This verse matters because it shows that kingdom-restoration expectation remains active even after Jesus' ministry and resurrection. The disciples are not speaking as though every communal and historical expectation has already vanished.

## Reasoning
The question is precise: will the kingdom be restored to Israel at this time? That makes the verse more than generic future hope. It preserves a concrete expectation about restoration, timing, and public consequence. Just as importantly, it does so after the resurrection. Whatever answer the surrounding passage gives, the question itself prevents an easy claim that the field had already become flat and exhausted.

## Local Significance
Locally, Acts 1:6 is important because it is post-resurrection. Expectation has survived remarkably far into the narrative. That does not prove an alternative fulfillment by itself, but it does show that restoration language remained a live category rather than a dead relic. The verse therefore gives the whole expectation layer historical depth as well as conceptual openness.

## Networked Significance
This page strengthens [Live Prophetic Expectation](../../../../../arguments/live-prophetic-expectation/summary.md) by proving that post-resurrection expectation is still historically present. It also helps [Anti-Closure Convergence](../../../../../convergences/anti-closure-convergence/summary.md), where the case depends on showing that the field cannot be declared closed too early.


## Contribution Summary
Acts 1:6 strengthens the thesis in support rank. It does not identify the later figure, but it keeps the expectation field alive at a very late point in the narrative and therefore makes premature closure harder to defend.
